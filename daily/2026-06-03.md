## [Announcing Spanner Graph Algorithms: Google-Grade Intelligence for Connected Data](https://cloud.google.com/blog/products/databases/introducing-spanner-graph-algorithms) 🤓

Spanner Graph algorithms bring native graph mining to Google Cloud's distributed database, eliminating the ETL pipelines and external graph engines typically required for structural analytics at scale. The feature supports 15+ algorithms covering centrality (PageRank, betweenness, closeness), community detection (modularity clustering, label propagation, weakly connected components), and similarity/path-finding — all invocable directly via ISO GQL within Spanner queries. Execution runs on dedicated Data Boost compute isolated from transactional workloads, processing billion-edge graphs within minutes; early adopters include SoundCloud (500M+ tracks, batch processes collapsed from hours to real-time) and Yahoo!'s Unified User Profile serving billions of users. #db #distributed #architecture #engineering

## [AlloyDB Hot Standby: Faster Failovers, Consistent Performance](https://cloud.google.com/blog/products/databases/alloydb-hot-standby-faster-failovers-and-consistent-performance) 👨‍💼

AlloyDB's high-availability upgrade from passive to Hot Standby eliminates the database-startup phase that caused performance brownouts after failover: the standby now runs PostgreSQL continuously, applying WAL records from the primary so its memory caches are warm at the moment it promotes. Failure detection completes within 30 seconds, failover finishes in roughly 15 seconds, and TPS returns to pre-failover levels almost immediately — versus several minutes of ramp-up under the legacy model. Hot Standby ships at no additional cost and is the default for new AlloyDB instances on PostgreSQL 18. #db #cloud #engineering #architecture

## [Architecting Cloud-Native Kafka: From Tiered Storage Towards a Diskless Future](https://www.infoq.com/articles/architecting-cloud-native-kafka/) 🤓

Kafka's cloud-native evolution splits into two distinct paths: Tiered Storage (KIP-405), which asynchronously offloads local log segments to remote storage while keeping brokers stateful, and the emerging diskless proposals — KIP-1150, KIP-1176, and KIP-1183 — which shift durability entirely to object storage and treat broker-local disks as ephemeral caches only. The article maps how FinOps telemetry, elastic consumer scaling, virtual clusters, and Share Groups each reshape the operational and economic model of event streaming, with Tiered Storage as the production-proven path today and the diskless proposals as the longer horizon. Three competing diskless KIPs reflect the community has not yet converged on a single design, making architectural bets on new clusters a calculated gamble on which standard will prevail. #distributed #architecture #engineering #cloud
