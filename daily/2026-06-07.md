## [30+ Updates per Second per Account: Uber Scales Ledger Processing with Batching](https://www.infoq.com/news/2026/06/uber-payment-batching-system/) 🤓

Uber's payment ledger faces a classic hot-account write-contention problem: high-volume driver or merchant accounts receive 30+ financial update operations per second, making individual round-trip-per-write untenable at scale. The User Account Batch Processing system amortizes data-store round trips by grouping writes for the same account into 250ms time windows coordinated via Redis — chosen for its sub-millisecond latency — and flushing results via optimistic atomic updates that preserve strict consistency and full auditability without specialized hardware. A three-service pipeline (Batch Creator, Batch Processor, Batch Post-Processing) collapsed what were previously multi-hour accounting runs into minutes. #distributed #db #engineering #architecture

## [AWS Replaces Fat-Tree Data Center Networks with Random Graph Theory, Cutting Routers by 69%](https://www.infoq.com/news/2026/06/aws-random-graph-data-center/) 🤓

Amazon has replaced fat-tree topologies in most new non-GPU data centers with Resilient Network Graphs (RNG) — a flat architecture derived from random graph theory where every top-of-rack switch can reach every other ToR through passive optical ShuffleBoxes, creating quasi-random inter-rack mesh connectivity without powered switching electronics. The custom Spraypoint routing protocol exploits RNG's extreme path diversity by spraying packets across multiple simultaneous next-hops rather than computing deterministic paths, and uses designated waypoints for long-distance traversal. The topological shift cuts router count by 69%, raises throughput 33%, and drops network power 40%; crucially, the random graph's lack of structural bottlenecks means losing 1% of routers degrades capacity by roughly 1% rather than triggering the cascading partition failures characteristic of fat-tree hierarchies. #architecture #cloud #engineering #distributed

## [The 3 Hidden Fallacies of Distributed Systems (Part 2)](https://thearchitectsnotebook.substack.com/p/the-3-hidden-fallacies-of-distributed) 👨‍💼

The final three of the 8 Fallacies of Distributed Computing are organizational rather than physical: they describe implicit trust and coordination assumptions that teams carry wholesale from monolithic systems into microservice architectures. The most operationally dangerous is the "Castle and Moat" model — treating shared VPC membership as authorization means a single SSRF in a public image-upload service becomes a direct pivot to any internal API. Mitigation requires zero-trust defaults: mTLS between every service pair, network policies scoped at the pod level, and treating intra-service traffic with the same skepticism applied at the perimeter. #distributed #architecture #softwareengineering #engineering
